The Fire

Australia has always burned. For millennia, fire shaped this continent — its plants evolved to survive it, its soils to regenerate after it. But over the last three decades, the relationship between this country and fire has broken down. Temperatures are rising. Droughts are intensifying. The fire season now starts earlier, ends later, and burns hotter than at any point in recorded history. What the data shows is not a natural cycle. It is a system under escalating pressure — and the pressure is not easing.

Chart 01 — Historical Fire Extent

Total Area Burned Across Australia, 1999–2024

The 2019–20 Black Summer dwarfs all previous seasons on record, burning more than double any prior year. The 2023–24 season eclipsed even that figure, driven by record-breaking heat and drought across northern and western Australia.

Source: Australian Bureau of Agricultural and Resource Economics / Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry Forest Fire Area Data; Australasian Fire and Emergency Service Authorities Council Annual Season Reviews

Chart 02 — Fire Footprint Map

Where Australia Burns: Fire Pressure Normalised by State Area (2016–2021)

Why normalise? A raw fire-area map mainly shows which states are physically large. This map divides burned hectares by state area, so smaller places with intense fire pressure are visible rather than being hidden by the size of Western Australia or Queensland.

The Northern Territory still shows high fire pressure because savanna landscapes burn often. The more important ecological warning is in the south-east: New South Wales and Victoria hold smaller land areas but far denser habitats, so concentrated fire there can remove refuges that species cannot easily replace.

Source: Australian Bureau of Agricultural and Resource Economics Forest Fire Area Data 2016–2021

Chart 03 — Climate Drivers

The Acceleration: Temperature, Rainfall & Fire Across 25 Fire Seasons

Each point is one fire season traced chronologically. As temperature anomalies rise and rainfall deficits deepen, area burned surges. Black Summer sits at the extreme end of every axis — a convergence of every climate driver simultaneously reaching record levels.

Source: Bureau of Meteorology — Climate Data; Australian Bureau of Agricultural and Resource Economics fire records

Chart 04 — Fire Danger Index

Dangerous Fire Weather Days by State and Month (2000–2024 Average)

The Forest Fire Danger Index combines temperature, humidity, wind speed and drought into a single risk measure. The Northern Territory burns year-round through tropical savanna; southern states face compressed but intensifying summer peaks growing more extreme each decade.

Source: Bureau of Meteorology — Forest Fire Danger Index records 2000–2024

“It wasn’t just a fire season. It was a reckoning.”

Black Summer wasn’t a one-off — it was the moment the data stopped looking like weather and started looking like a system collapsing. Hotter air, drier soil, longer fire windows: every driver you just saw moved in the same direction at the same time. The next pages show what that convergence cost in living things, not hectares.

02

The Collision

Before the first ember fell in the summer of 2019, Australia was already in ecological crisis. Decades of habitat clearing, drought, and invasive species had pushed hundreds of animals to the margins of survival. Then Black Summer came — the largest and hottest fire event ever recorded on this continent — and it burned directly through the last remaining refuges of species that had nowhere else to go. The collision between fire and biodiversity was not accidental. It was the predictable result of two crises arriving at the same moment.

Chart 07 — Threatened Species Map

Threatened Species Density by State and Territory

Normalised map: The colour shows threatened species per 10,000 square kilometres, not the absolute number of species. This avoids rewarding large states simply for being large and reveals where biodiversity pressure is most concentrated.

Queensland and New South Wales have the largest totals, but density changes the story: Tasmania and Victoria appear much more exposed once state area is accounted for. Hover over each state or territory to see the breakdown by threat category.

Source: Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water — Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act Threatened Species List 2024; International Union for Conservation of Nature Red List

“Five years on. The fires are out. The damage is not.”

Ecological recovery doesn’t run on a news cycle. The hollow-bearing trees that gliders nest in take a century to form. Alpine ash needs decades between fires to mature. The next pages track the populations that haven’t bounced back, the funding that hasn’t closed the gap, and the fire seasons climate models say are still coming.

03

The Aftermath

Five years after Black Summer, the landscape is beginning to look green again. But ecological recovery is measured in decades, not seasons. The hollow-bearing trees that greater gliders depend on can take a century to form. Alpine ash forests need decades between fires to mature. Meanwhile, the climate projections are unambiguous: without significant emissions reductions, fire seasons of Black Summer's scale become far more likely within the lifetime of species already struggling to recover. The window for meaningful intervention is open — but it will not stay open indefinitely.

Chart 09 — Species Recovery

Population Recovery Trajectory: Key Species Post–Black Summer (2020–2024)

Recovery is deeply uneven across affected species. Some are rebounding. Others remain critically below pre-fire population levels five years on.

Source: Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water Species Recovery Plans; World Wildlife Fund Australia post-fire monitoring reports

Chart 10 — Habitat Loss

Cumulative Forest & Woodland Loss by State (1990–2023)

Decades of land clearing combined with accelerating fire has pushed habitat loss to critical levels in every state. The curves show no sign of flattening.

Source: Australian Bureau of Agricultural and Resource Economics — Australia's State of the Forests Report 2023

Chart 11 — Funding vs Extinction Rate

Is Conservation Funding Making a Difference?

Despite tripling conservation funding after Black Summer, the threatened species list continues to grow. Money alone cannot reverse the scale of what was lost — or what the climate will keep taking.

Source: Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water Budget Statements / Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act Threatened Species List historical data

Chart 12 — Fire Eras

Fire Footprint by State Across Five Fire Eras

Black Summer was defined by an unprecedented concentration of fire in the ecologically rich south-east — a region that had burned relatively little in prior decades. The pattern is shifting southward as the climate dries.

Source: Australian Bureau of Agricultural and Resource Economics / Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry fire extent data; Australasian Fire and Emergency Service Authorities Council Annual Season Reviews 2000–2024

Chart 13 — Projections

Projected Catastrophic Fire Weather Days to 2070

Under high emissions, catastrophic fire weather days more than double by 2070. Under low emissions, the increase is still significant — but far smaller. The gap between these lines represents the cost of inaction, measured in fire seasons. Use the scenario selector to compare futures.

Source: Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation & Bureau of Meteorology — State of the Climate 2022; Climate Council of Australia projections

Chart 14 — Projected Fire Risk Map

Australia in 2070: Projected Fire Risk Under High Emissions

Reading the hotspots: Dot colour and size both represent projected severity. The largest red dots are places where repeat fire is most likely to arrive before ecosystems have had time to recover.

Under high emissions scenarios, Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation modelling projects sharply increased fire risk across southern and eastern Australia by 2070. Regions that have historically experienced moderate fire pressure — including Victoria, New South Wales, and South Australia — face the steepest relative increases. The north remains extreme; the south catches up. Hover over a hotspot to see the projected risk explanation.

Source: Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation & Bureau of Meteorology — State of the Climate 2022; Climate Council of Australia

What Remains

The fire is not the end of the story. Extinction is.

Every species lost to extinction is gone forever. Not endangered. Not recoverable. Gone.

Australia has already crossed that line more times than any other nation on Earth. Black Summer did not start this crisis — it accelerated one that has been building for decades. The fires are becoming more frequent. The recovery windows are shrinking. The species left have fewer places to go.

We are not watching a natural disaster unfold. We are watching the consequences of decisions — about emissions, about land clearing, about how much we are willing to spend to protect what remains.

The fires will come back. The only question is what will still be here when they do.
