01

The Fire

Australia has always burned. But something fundamental has changed — fires are growing bigger, hotter, and arriving earlier with every decade. This is not a natural cycle. This is acceleration.

Chart 01 — Historical Fire Extent

Total Area Burned Across Australia, 1999–2024

The 2019–20 Black Summer dwarfs all previous seasons on record, burning more than double any prior year. The 2023–24 season eclipsed even that figure, driven by record-breaking heat and drought across northern and western Australia.

Source: ABARES / DAFF Forest Fire Area Data; AFAC Annual Season Reviews

Chart 02 — Fire Footprint Map

Where Australia Burns: Cumulative Fire Area by State (2016–2021)

The Northern Territory and Western Australia carry the largest burned area through annual savanna fire. But it is the concentrated fires of NSW and Victoria — tearing through ecologically rich temperate forests — that caused the most catastrophic biodiversity impact during Black Summer.

Source: ABARES Forest Fire Area Data 2016–2021

Chart 03 — Climate Drivers

The Acceleration: Temperature, Rainfall & Fire Across 25 Fire Seasons

Each point is one fire season traced chronologically. As temperature anomalies rise and rainfall deficits deepen, area burned surges. Black Summer sits at the extreme end of every axis — a convergence of every climate driver simultaneously reaching record levels.

Source: Bureau of Meteorology — Climate Data; ABARES fire records

Chart 04 — Fire Danger Index

Dangerous Fire Weather Days by State and Month (2000–2024 Average)

The Forest Fire Danger Index combines temperature, humidity, wind speed and drought into a single risk measure. The Northern Territory burns year-round through tropical savanna; southern states face compressed but intensifying summer peaks growing more extreme each decade.

Source: Bureau of Meteorology — FFDI records 2000–2024

“It wasn’t just a fire season. It was a reckoning.”

Prof. Andy Pitman, ARC Centre of Excellence for Climate Extremes — on the conditions that defined Act I: fire weather so extreme it had no historical precedent in Australia.

02

The Collision

Australia is already the world’s worst nation for mammal extinctions. Then Black Summer came — and burned straight through the habitats of species already on the edge. The fire didn’t just destroy land. It destroyed futures.

Chart 06 — Species at Risk by State

Threatened Species Count vs Fire Habitat Overlap

NSW and QLD host the most threatened species and were the worst fire-affected states. Bar colour shows what percentage of each state's critical habitat overlapped the 2019–20 fire footprint.

Source: DCCEEW — EPBC Act Threatened Species List 2024

Chart 07 — Threatened Species Map

Australia's Biodiversity Crisis: EPBC-Listed Threatened Species by State

Queensland and New South Wales are home to the greatest concentration of threatened species in Australia. Hover over each state to see the breakdown of Critically Endangered, Endangered and Vulnerable species.

Source: DCCEEW — EPBC Act Threatened Species List 2024; IUCN Red List

Chart 08 — Escalating Risk

How Black Summer Changed the Threat Status of Key Species

After Black Summer, 16 species across five taxonomic groups were formally uplisted to higher threat categories under the EPBC Act. Each line is a species. Each downward slope is a step closer to extinction. Use the filter to explore by group.

Source: DCCEEW / IUCN Red List — post-fire species assessments 2020–2022

“Five years on. The fires are out. The damage is not.”

The reality entering Act III — recovery is uneven, the threatened species list keeps growing, and the climate trajectory that caused Black Summer remains on course to repeat it.

03

The Aftermath

Some ecosystems are beginning to recover. Others will not. And the climate models are unambiguous — unless emissions trajectories change dramatically, fire seasons like 2019–20 will become the new normal.

Chart 09 — Species Recovery

Population Recovery Trajectory: Key Species Post–Black Summer (2020–2024)

Recovery is deeply uneven across affected species. Some are rebounding. Others remain critically below pre-fire population levels five years on.

Source: DCCEEW Species Recovery Plans; WWF Australia post-fire monitoring reports

Chart 10 — Habitat Loss

Cumulative Forest & Woodland Loss by State (1990–2023)

Decades of land clearing combined with accelerating fire has pushed habitat loss to critical levels in every state. The curves show no sign of flattening.

Source: ABARES — Australia's State of the Forests Report 2023

Chart 11 — Funding vs Extinction Rate

Is Conservation Funding Making a Difference?

Despite tripling conservation funding after Black Summer, the threatened species list continues to grow. Money alone cannot reverse the scale of what was lost — or what the climate will keep taking.

Source: DCCEEW Budget Statements / EPBC Act Threatened Species List historical data

Chart 12 — Fire Eras

Fire Footprint by State Across Five Fire Eras

Black Summer was defined by an unprecedented concentration of fire in the ecologically rich south-east — a region that had burned relatively little in prior decades. The pattern is shifting southward as the climate dries.

Source: ABARES / DAFF fire extent data; AFAC Annual Season Reviews 2000–2024

Chart 13 — Projections

Projected Catastrophic Fire Weather Days to 2070

Under high emissions, catastrophic fire weather days more than double by 2070. Under low emissions, the increase is still significant — but manageable. The gap between these lines represents the cost of inaction, measured in fire seasons. Toggle the checkboxes to compare scenarios.

Source: CSIRO & BOM — State of the Climate 2022; Climate Council of Australia projections

Chart 14 — Projected Fire Risk Map

Australia in 2070: Projected Fire Risk Under High Emissions

Under high emissions scenarios, CSIRO modelling projects dramatically increased fire risk across southern and eastern Australia by 2070. States that have historically experienced moderate fire pressure — Victoria, NSW, and South Australia — face the steepest relative increases. The north remains extreme; the south catches up. Hover over each state to see the projected risk index.

Source: CSIRO & BOM — State of the Climate 2022; Climate Council of Australia
